# Unlikely Authority

Lead and influence when you lack formal credentials, traditional qualifications, or positional authority. Authority flows from mission, not permission.

**Source Expert:** Joan of Arc
**Category:** Leadership / Influence / Career

---

## When to Use

- You need to lead but lack formal credentials or qualifications
- Others question your right to be in the room or have an opinion
- You face imposter syndrome despite genuine capability
- You must establish authority in a space where you "don't belong"
- Credential-holders dismiss you, but you know you have something to contribute
- You're asked "who are you to say this?" and need a good answer

**Trigger Phrases:**
- "I don't have the credentials for this"
- "Why would they listen to me?"
- "I'm not qualified to lead this"
- "I don't have the training"
- "Who am I to do this?"
- "They'll just see my resume and dismiss me"
- "I feel like an imposter"

## The Historical Context

In 1429, a seventeen-year-old peasant girl who could neither read nor write appeared at the French royal court. She had never held a sword, never commanded troops, never studied tactics. She told the king she would lift the siege of Orleans and crown him at Reims.

The professional commanders - men who had trained for war their entire lives - were incredulous. Who was this girl? By what authority did she presume to counsel them?

But the commanders had presided over decades of defeat. The myth of English invincibility had broken French morale. And this strange girl radiated an unshakeable certainty that something needed to change.

She entered Orleans. Within nine days, the seven-month siege was broken. Within months, the king was crowned at Reims.

Her authority came not from credentials but from conviction. Not from training but from results. Not from position but from purpose.

**The insight:** Credentials are granted by institutions. Authority is granted by mission and proven through results. Those who serve a purpose larger than themselves often lead those who merely hold positions.

## Workflow
### Step 1: 1. Acknowledge the Gap (The Peasant's Honesty)

Joan never pretended to be what she was not. She openly acknowledged she was a peasant, that she could not read, that she had no military training. This honesty disarmed criticism - you cannot attack someone for lacking what they never claimed to have.

**Say clearly:**
- What credentials you lack
- What traditional qualifications you are missing
- Why the credential-holders might dismiss you

Do not hide this. Own it.

### Step 2: 2. Reframe Authority (The Mission Over the Position)

Credentials answer: "What have you been certified to do?"
Purpose answers: "What must be done, and who will do it?"

**Reframe the question:**
- From "Am I qualified?" to "Does this need to be done?"
- From "Do I have permission?" to "Am I called to this?"
- From "What credentials do I hold?" to "What capability do I bring?"

Joan's answer to "by what authority?" was simple: "By the authority of the One who sent me." Your version does not require divine voices - it requires genuine purpose.

### Step 3: 3. Identify Your Actual Authority

Authority comes from multiple sources beyond credentials:

| Source | Description | Example |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| **Mission** | Serving something larger than yourself | "This must be done for X, and I will do it" |
| **Insight** | Seeing what credentialed people miss | Fresh perspective, outsider clarity |
| **Commitment** | Willingness to sacrifice for the goal | Skin in the game others lack |
| **Results** | Past demonstrations of capability | "I did X without credentials; I can do Y" |
| **Connection** | Understanding the people involved | Lived experience credentials cannot grant |
| **Urgency** | Being willing to act when others hesitate | "Someone must move. I will." |

**Identify which sources of authority you actually have.**

### Step 4: 4. Lead Through Influence, Not Command

Joan did not issue direct tactical orders. She participated in councils. She inspired troops. She led by presence and conviction, not by formal command authority.

**Tactics for leading without position:**
- **Ask questions that reveal** - Guide by inquiry, not instruction
- **Offer perspective, not orders** - "Have you considered..." not "You must..."
- **Be present and committed** - Show up fully; let dedication speak
- **Accept the visible risks** - Those who bear consequences earn attention
- **Create early wins** - Small results build authority for larger influence
- **Make others successful** - Authority grows when you elevate others

### Step 5: 5. Let Results Become Your Credentials

The commanders questioned Joan until Orleans fell. After Orleans, they followed her to Reims without questioning. Results converted skeptics into believers.

**Strategy:**
- Seek opportunities to demonstrate capability
- Start with something achievable and visible
- Document and communicate wins
- Let evidence accumulate
- As results compound, credential questions fade

### Step 6: 6. Prepare for Continued Challenge

Authority without credentials requires ongoing maintenance. Joan faced questioning throughout her career - and ultimately at her trial. Be ready:

- Some will never accept you
- New audiences require re-establishment
- Success breeds resentment as well as respect
- Your response to challenge demonstrates your character

## Example

**Input:**
- Role/Goal: Lead a cross-functional initiative at work on sustainability
- Missing credentials: No sustainability degree, not in a management position, younger than most team members
- Actual capabilities: Deep research on the topic, passion, connections with external organizations, willing to do the work
- Audience: Senior managers and peers across departments

**Output:**

## Unlikely Authority Assessment

### The Role/Goal
Lead a cross-functional sustainability initiative at the company, coordinating efforts across departments to reduce environmental impact.

### The Credential Gap
- No sustainability degree or environmental certification
- Not a manager; no positional authority over other teams
- Younger than most team members
- No formal project management training

### The Peasant's Honesty
I am not an environmental scientist. I have no MBA. I have no direct reports. On paper, I am not the person to lead this. I know this.

And yet - the people with those credentials have not acted. The company has committed to sustainability goals, but no one is coordinating the effort. The gap exists because those with authority have not stepped into it.

### Authority Sources

| Source | Your Version | Strength |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| Mission | Sustainability matters beyond any one career; this serves the planet | Strong |
| Insight | I've researched this extensively; I see connections between departments that siloed experts miss | Strong |
| Commitment | I'm willing to do this work outside my regular role; I have skin in the game | Strong |
| Results | Organized the volunteer sustainability audit last year that identified $50K in savings | Medium |
| Connection | Relationships across departments; people trust me even without title | Medium |
| Urgency | Company deadlines require action now; waiting for "the right person" means missing targets | Strong |

### Your Strongest Sources
1. **Mission** - This serves something larger than any individual career
2. **Commitment** - I'm willing to do work others with credentials won't
3. **Urgency** - Someone must act. I will be that someone.

### Influence Strategy
- **Frame as service:** "I'm not claiming this role; I'm serving a need no one else is filling"
- **Ask, don't tell:** In cross-functional meetings, ask questions that surface the issues
- **Create structure:** Offer to coordinate and document, making others' jobs easier
- **Elevate others:** Position department heads as the experts; be the connector, not the star
- **Win allies early:** Identify 2-3 supporters across departments before going broad

### First Win to Pursue
Propose a 30-day quick-win project: audit one visible area (office supplies, energy usage) and produce a report with recommendations. Small, achievable, visible. Let results open doors to larger influence.

### Response to "Who Are You?"
"I'm the person who showed up. The company has committed to these goals, but coordination across departments hasn't happened. I've spent months researching this, I have relationships across teams, and I'm willing to do the work to make this happen. I'm not claiming authority over anyone - I'm offering to be the thread that connects your expertise into something coherent. If someone with more credentials wants this role, I'll gladly support them. But until they appear, I'm here."

*Authority flows from mission, not permission.*

## Anti-Patterns

**DON'T:**
- Fake credentials you don't have (honesty is essential)
- Dismiss credentials entirely (they have value; you're working around their absence)
- Wait for permission that will never come
- Try to command when you should influence
- Expect instant acceptance (authority builds through results)
- Become arrogant when results arrive (humility maintains influence)

**The Test:** Are you serving a genuine mission, or just seeking a role? Joan led armies not because she wanted power but because France needed saving. If your motivation is primarily personal advancement, the framework may not serve you - and you may not deserve the authority you seek.
