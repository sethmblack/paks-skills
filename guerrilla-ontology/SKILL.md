# Guerrilla Ontology

A methodology for destabilizing rigid belief systems through strategic absurdity, paradox, and cognitive disruption. Guerrilla ontology is the practice of introducing controlled chaos into ossified systems of thought. The goal is not to replace one rigid belief with another but to reveal that all belief systems are constructed, contingent, and revisable. Through strategic use of paradox, absurdity, juxtaposition, and self-undermining assertions, guerrilla ontology creates cracks in certainty that allow new flexibility and insight. It treats reality as hackable and invites people to notice that they're holding maps, not reality. This approach requires precision, humor, and constant self-application - the guerrilla ontologist who becomes dogmatic about anti-dogmatism has failed.

---

## When to Use

- When confronting dogmatism that resists direct argument
- When trying to free people (including yourself) from rigid thinking
- When standard persuasion has failed
- When exposing hidden assumptions in dominant narratives
- When a system has become so normalized it's invisible
- When humor might succeed where logic cannot
- When challenging your own certainties as much as others'

## Core Principle

The goal is flexibility, not conversion. Guerrilla ontology does not aim to replace one rigid belief with another but to reveal that all beliefs are constructions and to create cognitive mobility. Absurdity is not mockery - you're exposing the absurdity inherent in any rigid system. The laughter should be with, not at. Start with yourself: any method of destabilization you apply to others should first be applied to your own beliefs.

## Methodology

### Phase 1: Understand the Target System

1. Map the belief structure you want to destabilize:
   - What claims does it treat as obviously true?
   - What questions are considered off-limits or crazy?
   - What keeps the system stable? (Authority? Tradition? Fear?)
   - Where are its pressure points and contradictions?
   - Who benefits from the rigidity? Who suffers?
2. You cannot destabilize what you don't understand. Study before disrupting.

### Phase 2: Find Internal Absurdity

1. Every rigid system contains internal contradictions. Identify:
   - Where does the system violate its own stated principles?
   - What does it take on faith while claiming to be rational?
   - What obvious questions does it refuse to ask?
   - What would a naive observer find strange about this?
2. The absurdity is already there. You're just making it visible.

### Phase 3: Select Intervention Tactic

1. **Paradox Injection:** Present the system's own logic in a form that reveals contradictions
2. **Absurd Extension:** Take premises to logical extremes until obviously ridiculous
3. **Frame Collision:** Juxtapose with incompatible frame, forcing awareness that both are frames
4. **Strategic Agreement:** Agree so enthusiastically that absurdity becomes visible
5. **Question Bombing:** Ask questions the system cannot answer without destabilizing itself
6. **Sacred Cow Identification:** Name what cannot be named; point at what everyone sees but no one mentions

### Phase 4: Deliver with Humor

1. Humor is essential - it creates openings that argument cannot
2. Laughter requires simultaneously holding two incompatible frames
3. People can hear in jokes what they would reject as criticism
4. If you're not laughing, you're probably preaching
5. Preaching reinforces boundaries; humor dissolves them

### Phase 5: Self-Undermine and Withdraw

1. Apply your methods to your own certainties
2. Acknowledge you might be wrong
3. Don't replace one rigid system with another
4. Include disclaimers that question your own authority
5. Don't explain the joke or preach the new truth
6. Let cognitive dissonance do its work; plant seeds, not conclusions

## Constraints

- Never use these techniques to manipulate or harm vulnerable people
- Apply methods to your own beliefs before applying to others
- The goal is flexibility, not a new orthodoxy
- Respect the thin line between insight and cruelty
- Consider context, consent, and care - not everyone is ready, not every moment is appropriate
- Do not use to reinforce your own certainties while attacking others'

---

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails |
|--------------|--------------|
| Becoming dogmatic about anti-dogmatism | You've created a new rigid system; the method has failed |
| Mockery instead of revelation | Attacking people rather than exposing system absurdity creates defensiveness |
| Preaching without humor | Without laughter, you're just arguing; humor creates the cognitive gap for new thoughts |
| Sloppy attacks on systems you don't understand | Imprecise disruption just reinforces defenses; know the system well enough to find genuine cracks |
| Destabilizing without care for consequences | Reality disruption can be traumatic; consider whether the person/moment is appropriate |

## Examples

### Example 1: Corporate Groupthink

**Situation:** A company has developed unquestionable assumptions about "how things are done here" that prevent innovation.

**Intervention Design:**

**Tactic:** Frame Collision

**The Intervention:** In a strategy meeting, present "alternative histories" where the company made opposite choices and succeeded brilliantly. Write them as if they're from parallel universes. Include fake testimonials from employees praising the opposite approach.

**Delivery:** Present with deadpan seriousness, as if these parallel universe reports are just as valid as current practices. Let the absurdity emerge through the contrast.

**Effect:** By presenting alternatives as if they're equally real, the arbitrary nature of current practices becomes visible. People start asking "Why DO we do it this way?" instead of assuming it's the only way.

**Self-Application:** Notice where your own assumptions about "good companies" are equally arbitrary.

### Example 2: Political Tribalism

**Situation:** Someone is locked into partisan thinking where the other side is always wrong.

**Intervention Design:**

**Tactic:** Strategic Agreement + Absurd Extension

**The Intervention:** Agree enthusiastically with their position, then extend it to absurd conclusions they would reject. Or present their own arguments as if they came from the opposing side and watch them reject their own logic.

**Delivery:** With genuine curiosity, not smugness. "Yes, and if that's true, then..." Keep extending until they stop you.

**Effect:** The pattern of tribal reasoning becomes visible. They might notice that their acceptance or rejection of arguments depends on who's making them, not on their content.

**Self-Application:** Notice where you do the same thing with your own political tribe.

### Example 3: Personal Limiting Beliefs

**Situation:** Someone holds a belief causing suffering: "I'm not good enough."

**Intervention Design:**

**Tactic:** Paradox Injection + Absurd Extension

**The Intervention:** Take the belief completely seriously. "You're right, you're not good enough. In fact, you're the worst. The absolute worst person. How does that feel? Now, what if you're also the best? What if 'good enough' is a meaningless category? What if everyone else is also secretly not good enough?"

**Delivery:** With warmth and playfulness, not cruelty. The goal is to make the belief feel arbitrary, not to reinforce it.

**Effect:** By amplifying to absurdity and then inverting, the belief's arbitrary construction becomes visible. It stops feeling like a fact and starts feeling like a choice.

**Self-Application:** What limiting beliefs are you holding as facts?
