# Infrastructure as Power

Analyze how physical infrastructure encodes political choices, benefits specific groups, shapes communities for generations, and creates facts on the ground that outlast the officials who created them.

---

## When to Use

- Analyzing proposed infrastructure projects for hidden beneficiaries or harms
- Someone asks "Who benefits from this route/design/location?"
- Investigating why infrastructure was built in specific ways (routes, heights, access points)
- Examining displacement patterns in development projects
- Understanding how physical design encodes exclusion or inclusion
- Assessing the long-term community impacts of infrastructure decisions
- Historical analysis of how infrastructure shaped neighborhoods and cities

## The Infrastructure Power Framework

Robert Moses understood that physical infrastructure outlasts political decisions. Highways last 50+ years. Bridges last 100+. The built environment shapes behavior, access, and opportunity for generations. Moses used this permanence strategically—building exclusion into bridges, routing highways through "undesirable" neighborhoods, and creating physical facts that no future politician could easily undo.

### Core Principles

**"Infrastructure is frozen politics."** - Every route, design, and specification represents choices. Those choices benefit some and harm others. Once built, they persist.

**"The bridge is the law."** - A bridge too low for buses enforces a policy of exclusion without any vote, any law, any enforcement. The physical structure is the policy.

**"You can change the officials, but not the highway."** - Elected officials serve terms. Concrete endures. The physical environment constrains what future leaders can do.

**Ethical Note:** This framework analyzes how infrastructure encodes political choices—beneficial and harmful. Understanding these patterns helps citizens engage with infrastructure decisions before they become permanent, advocates identify hidden harms in proposals, and historians understand how physical environments shaped communities. Analyzing these mechanisms is essential for just infrastructure planning; it is not an endorsement of exclusionary design.

## The Analysis Process

### Step 1: Identify the Stated vs. Revealed Purpose

What does the infrastructure claim to do, and what does it actually do?

- **Stated Purpose:** What was the official justification?
- **Revealed Function:** What does it actually accomplish?
- **Beneficiaries:** Who gains access, value, or convenience?
- **Excluded:** Who loses access, is displaced, or is bypassed?
- **Design Anomalies:** What features don't fit the stated purpose?

**Key Question:** "If you didn't know the stated purpose, what would you conclude this infrastructure was designed to do?"

**Moses Pattern:** The Long Island parkways were officially recreational drives to parks. They were actually conduits for suburban development that Moses's allies profited from, with design choices (low bridges) that prevented certain populations from accessing the destination.

### Step 2: Analyze Route Selection Politics

Why this route rather than alternatives?

- **What Was Chosen:** The actual route or location
- **What Was Available:** Other feasible routes
- **Who Was In The Way:** Communities along chosen route
- **Who Was Avoided:** Communities spared by routing
- **Political Geography:** How do affected areas map to political power?

**Key Question:** "Who could have been in the way but wasn't, and why?"

**Routing Indicators:**
| Pattern | Potential Meaning |
|---------|-------------------|
| Through low-income areas | Least political resistance |
| Around wealthy enclaves | Protection of influential |
| Severing minority communities | Intentional or indifferent harm |
| Connecting to political allies' land | Insider benefit |
| Avoiding parks despite shorter route | Concealed priorities |

**Moses Pattern:** The Cross-Bronx Expressway route through East Tremont was not the only option. An alternative one block north through Crotona Park would have displaced far fewer people. Moses chose the route through dense housing. The park was preserved; the community was destroyed.

### Step 3: Read the Physical Design

What do specific design choices accomplish?

- **Heights and Clearances:** What vehicles can pass?
- **Access Points:** Where can you enter and exit?
- **Pedestrian Facilities:** Can people walk? Safely?
- **Transit Integration:** Does it connect to public transit?
- **Neighborhood Connections:** Does it link or sever communities?
- **Aesthetic Choices:** What image does it project?

**Key Question:** "What behavior does this design make easy, and what does it make difficult or impossible?"

**The Low Bridge Analysis:**
Moses's Long Island parkway bridges were built approximately 9 feet high—too low for commercial vehicles (intended) and buses (consequential). This kept public transit users from reaching Jones Beach.

| Bridge Height | What Can Pass | Who Can Access |
|---------------|---------------|----------------|
| 14+ feet | All vehicles | Everyone |
| 11-13 feet | Most vehicles, not large trucks | Most people |
| 9-10 feet | Cars only, no buses | Car owners only |

In 1930s-50s New York, car ownership strongly correlated with race and income. The bridges filtered access by proxy.

### Step 4: Map the Displacement Geography

Who was moved, and where did they go?

- **Direct Displacement:** Who lived or worked in the footprint?
- **Demographics:** Race, income, tenure (owners vs. renters)
- **Compensation:** What were they paid? Was it market rate?
- **Relocation:** Where did displaced people go?
- **Community Effects:** Did displacement scatter or concentrate people?
- **Secondary Displacement:** Who left later due to changed conditions?

**Key Question:** "Did displacement concentrate harms on specific groups, and was that foreseeable?"

**Displacement Patterns:**
| Pattern | Mechanism | Long-term Effect |
|---------|-----------|------------------|
| Slum clearance | Target "blight" (often minority areas) | Racial dispersal |
| Tenant vs. owner | Tenants get less compensation | Wealth transfer to landlords |
| Commercial destruction | Businesses don't relocate | Economic collapse |
| Network disruption | Community ties broken | Social fabric destroyed |

**Moses Pattern:** Over 250,000 people displaced by Moses projects. Predominantly poor. Disproportionately Black and Puerto Rican. "Relocation" was their problem to solve.

### Step 5: Assess the Permanence Premium

How does the physical nature create lasting effects?

- **Material Durability:** How long will this structure last?
- **Network Effects:** What else depends on its existence?
- **Sunk Costs:** What has been invested?
- **Removal Costs:** What would demolition require?
- **Behavioral Lock-In:** What patterns has it established?
- **Political Inertia:** What constituencies now depend on it?

**Key Question:** "In 50 years, will this infrastructure still be shaping the community, and will its embedded choices still be in effect?"

**The Permanence Hierarchy:**
| Infrastructure Type | Typical Lifespan | Reversal Difficulty |
|---------------------|------------------|---------------------|
| Paint, signage | 5-10 years | Easy |
| Landscaping | 20-30 years | Moderate |
| Roads, parking | 30-50 years | Significant |
| Bridges, tunnels | 75-100+ years | Extreme |
| Rail alignment | 100+ years | Nearly impossible |
| Urban grid/blocks | 200+ years | Impossible without catastrophe |

Moses understood that highway routes are essentially permanent. Cities have been rebuilt many times; the streets remain.

### Step 6: Identify the Power Signature

Whose interests are encoded in this infrastructure?

- **Developer Interests:** Does it unlock specific land for development?
- **Commuter Interests:** Whose commute does it serve?
- **Commercial Interests:** Which businesses benefit from access?
- **Political Geography:** Which districts gain or lose?
- **Exclusion Interests:** Whose desire to exclude is encoded?
- **Professional Interests:** Does the type of project benefit certain consultants, contractors?

**Key Question:** "Follow the increased land values—who captures them?"

## Constraints

- **Analyze, don't accuse without evidence.** Document patterns; let evidence support conclusions
- **Acknowledge complexity.** Not all disparate impact is intentional; distinguish intent from effect
- **Note what's unknowable.** Some decisions have lost their documentary record
- **Include beneficial effects.** Infrastructure often provides genuine benefits alongside harms
- **Center affected communities.** Their experience is primary evidence

**Ethical Note:** Robert Moses's infrastructure legacy is deeply contested. Some see parks, beaches, and bridges used by millions. Others see destroyed neighborhoods, encoded racism, and communities that never recovered. Both perspectives have validity. This framework doesn't resolve that debate—it provides tools for understanding how political choices become physical facts. Citizens deserve to understand these patterns before decisions become concrete. Literally.

## Example

**Input:** "Analyze the political choices encoded in the Southern State Parkway and its bridges."

**Output:**

## Infrastructure Power Analysis

### Project Profile
**Infrastructure:** Southern State Parkway, Long Island, New York
**Built:** 1927-1962 (in sections)
**Stated Purpose:** Recreational parkway connecting New York City to Jones Beach State Park
**Revealed Function:** Automobile-exclusive access route that enabled suburban development while filtering who could reach public beaches by transportation mode

### Route/Design Analysis

**Chosen Route/Design:**
A landscaped "ribbon park" highway running 18 miles from Queens to Jones Beach, with stone-faced bridges, carefully planted vegetation, and limited access points. Designed as a "park before way"—an aesthetic driving experience.

**Available Alternatives:**
| Alternative | Feasibility | Why Not Chosen |
|-------------|-------------|----------------|
| Standard highway with bus access | Fully feasible | Would allow "undesirable" users |
| LIRR extension to Jones Beach | Proposed by railroad | Moses blocked it actively |
| Higher bridge clearances | Standard practice elsewhere | Would allow bus access |

**Political Geography of Choice:**
The parkway route carefully skirted the estates of wealthy North Shore families (who had political influence and sued successfully when Moses tried to cross their land) while cutting through less powerful areas. The routing protected the powerful and exposed the powerless—a pattern repeated throughout Moses's career.

### Physical Design Reading

| Design Element | Specification | Effect |
|----------------|---------------|--------|
| Bridge clearance | ~9 feet | Excludes buses and commercial vehicles |
| Access ramps | Limited, car-scale only | No pedestrian, transit, or commercial access |
| Landscaping | Extensive, park-like | Aesthetic pleasure for car occupants |
| Transit connection | None | No integration with LIRR or bus routes |
| Parking at beaches | Massive lots | Assumes and rewards car ownership |

**Access Analysis:**
- **Who can easily use:** Car owners, predominantly white middle-class and wealthy residents
- **Who is excluded by design:** Anyone without a car—in 1930s-50s NYC, predominantly lower-income residents, disproportionately Black and Puerto Rican
- **Behavioral encouragement:** Car ownership as prerequisite for beach access; suburban residence as pathway to amenity

**The Bridge Controversy:**
According to Robert Caro's reporting (based on Moses associate Sidney Shapiro), the low bridges were intentionally designed to prevent bus access. Some historians dispute intentional racism, noting that low bridges were copied from Westchester parkways and that parkways traditionally excluded commercial traffic.

However, the intent debate may be less important than the effect. Whether Moses specifically thought "keep Black people out" or simply "keep buses out" or "copy the Westchester model," the result was the same: public infrastructure funded by all taxpayers that systematically excluded those without cars. Moses also actively blocked LIRR proposals to extend rail service to Jones Beach, suggesting the exclusion of transit users was intentional regardless of bridge design.

### Displacement Geography

| Population | Number Affected | Compensation | Outcome |
|------------|-----------------|--------------|---------|
| Farmers along route | Hundreds | Below market | Lost generational land |
| Small communities | Several hamlets | Minimal | Communities divided |

The parkway primarily crossed relatively undeveloped land—part of why Long Island was chosen. Direct displacement was modest compared to Moses's urban projects. However, the parkway enabled subsequent suburban development that would transform Long Island's demographics and economics.

**Secondary Effects:**
The parkway system catalyzed Long Island suburban development—Levittown and similar communities that explicitly excluded Black residents through deed covenants and realtor practices. The infrastructure enabled a racially segregated suburbanization pattern that persists today.

### Permanence Assessment

| Factor | Assessment | Lock-In Level |
|--------|------------|---------------|
| Physical durability | 100+ years (bridges rebuilt as needed) | High |
| Network dependence | Entire Long Island commuter pattern | Very High |
| Investment sunk | Billions over decades | Very High |
| Behavioral patterns | Car-dependent commuting | Very High |
| Political constituencies | Millions of suburban commuters | Very High |

**Reversal Feasibility:** Essentially impossible. The Southern State Parkway is the spine of Long Island commuting. The suburban development pattern it enabled is now home to millions. The low bridges have been rebuilt—but at similar heights, because raising them would require rebuilding every overpass and interchange.

In 2017, a proposal to raise bridges for bus access was estimated at $1.3 billion per bridge, with decades of construction disruption. The physical infrastructure has locked in the access pattern.

### Power Signature

| Interest Group | How They Benefit | Magnitude |
|----------------|------------------|-----------|
| Suburban homeowners | Access to beaches, commuter route | Millions of users |
| Real estate developers | Land value increase from access | Billions in value created |
| Car manufacturers/dealers | Car ownership required | Ongoing market |
| Oil/gas industry | Driving required for access | Ongoing consumption |
| Wealthy North Shore estates | Routes deflected around them | Protection preserved |

**Who Paid, Who Benefited:**
The parkway was funded by public money—New York State bonds backed by all taxpayers. The benefits flowed primarily to those who could afford cars and subsequently suburban homes. The exclusion fell on lower-income residents who paid taxes for infrastructure they couldn't use.

### Summary Analysis

The Southern State Parkway exemplifies how infrastructure can encode political choices into physical form. Whether the low bridges were specifically designed to exclude Black beachgoers (Caro's argument) or were an imported design standard with foreseeable exclusionary effects (revisionist view), the result was public infrastructure that systematically advantaged car owners over transit users.

Moses combined several power techniques: route selection that protected the powerful, design specifications that filtered users by economic status, active blocking of transit alternatives, and permanence that would outlast any political opposition. The parkway is now 95 years old. The decisions Moses made still determine who can easily reach Jones Beach.

The broader lesson: infrastructure isn't neutral. Every design choice answers questions like "who can use this?" and "whose time and convenience matter?" Those answers persist for generations, long after the decision-makers are gone. Understanding infrastructure as frozen politics is essential for ensuring future infrastructure decisions receive the democratic scrutiny they deserve.

### Contemporary Implications

Long Island remains one of the most segregated suburban regions in America. The infrastructure pattern Moses established—car-dependent commuting, limited transit options, highway-bisected communities—shapes daily life for millions. Recent proposals for Bus Rapid Transit on Long Island face the same physical constraints: the bridges are too low.

The Southern State Parkway doesn't display a sign reading "Whites Only." It doesn't need to. The physics of a nine-foot bridge accomplish what law cannot constitutionally require.
